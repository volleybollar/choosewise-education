The WISE Framework for Education

The right tool at the right time.

Four questions that turn any "should we use this digital tool?" conversation into a structured decision — grounded in professional judgment, not vendor hype or political opinions. The WISE framework can be used for all tools, analog and digital, so don't see it as a model that should only be used when it comes to AI.

Notice that the third step is colored differently. That's deliberate: Weigh, Inspect and Evaluate are reflective steps — Select is the decision moment, where professional judgment becomes a concrete choice.

W — Weigh the learning goal

What should students learn? What prior knowledge do they have? Always start with pedagogy — never with the tool.

I — Inspect what the subject requires

What does this particular subject and content call for? An essay in language arts has different tool needs than a problem-solving task in mathematics.

S — Select the right tool

Choose the tool that best serves learning for the students you have: pen and paper, a physical book, a digital book, other digital tools, an AI tool, no tool at all, or something entirely different?

E — Evaluate the outcome

Did it work well? Did students learn better with this tool than they would have with a different one? Adjust and run the cycle again.

One important exception

There is one exception where a teacher should choose a different tool than the one the WISE Framework recommends — and that's when the tool that produces the best learning for students also produces negative consequences. Those consequences may apply to individual students or to the whole class. This is where the teacher's professional judgment comes in: the teacher must weigh pros and cons against each other before making a well-grounded decision.

The WISE Framework for School Leaders

W — Weigh the objective

What problem should this initiative solve? Start with the school's vision and the needs of students and teachers — not with the tech budget or the latest tool.

I — Investigate subjects and operations

Which subjects and activities benefit from this initiative — and which don't? An investment that works in mathematics may not work in language arts. Conduct thorough needs assessments that lead to well-founded decisions!

S — Select with strategic intent

Which tools should the school invest in — and which should it actively choose not to? Which of the tools currently available deliver the desired results and which don't?

E — Evaluate with data

Set evaluation criteria before the initiative is implemented — not afterwards. Did student outcomes improve? Did teacher competence increase? Use data to evaluate and discontinue what doesn't work.

Common questions about the WISE Framework

What is the WISE Framework for Education?

WISE is a four-step decision model — Weigh, Inspect, Select, Evaluate — that helps teachers and school leaders decide whether (and which) tool fits a specific learning goal. It puts professional judgment at the centre rather than the technology itself, and works for any tool, analog or digital — not only AI.

Who is the WISE Framework for?

Teachers, principals, school administrators, and education leaders. WISE is presented in two layers — a classroom version focused on learning outcomes for individual lessons, and a leadership version focused on whole-school strategy, procurement, and evaluation.

How is WISE different from other AI-in-school models?

WISE assumes the right answer is sometimes "no AI". The goal is matching tool to learning, not adopting tools per se. It is also explicitly written for the realities of running a Swedish school — curriculum logic, GDPR, and teacher workload — but the structure transfers to any school system.

Is the WISE Framework only for AI tools?

No. WISE evaluates any pedagogical tool — pen-and-paper exercises, textbooks, digital platforms, AI assistants, or no tool at all. AI is one option on the menu, not the default. That neutrality is part of why teachers can use it without a stance for or against AI.
